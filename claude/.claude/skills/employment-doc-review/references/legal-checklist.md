# NC + Federal Legal Checklist — Employee-Side Review

Work through every applicable section. Items marked **(defect check)** can make
a clause or the whole release legally vulnerable — that's negotiating leverage,
so flag them prominently. Statute references below are stable anchors; where a
question turns on recent case law or agency action (noted inline), verify the
current state before asserting enforceability.

## 1. Release of claims (the heart of most separation agreements)

- **Consideration**: Is the employee getting something beyond what they're
  already owed? Payment of earned wages/PTO is NOT consideration — they're
  entitled to that regardless. **(defect check)**
- **ADEA / OWBPA — employee age 40+** **(defect check)**: A release of age
  discrimination claims is invalid unless it:
  - is written in plain language and specifically references the ADEA;
  - advises the employee in writing to consult an attorney;
  - gives **21 days** to consider (individual) or **45 days** (group/exit
    program, which also requires a written disclosure of the decisional unit:
    job titles and ages of those selected and not selected);
  - gives **7 days after signing to revoke**, and takes effect only after
    that window closes.
  - Check the math: if the document was delivered late or the deadline is
    shorter than required, say so — the age release may be unenforceable.
- **Claims that cannot be waived** — flag any language purporting to release:
  - the right to **file a charge or participate in an EEOC/NLRB/SEC
    investigation** (waiving monetary recovery from a charge is generally
    permissible; waiving the right to file is not);
  - **FLSA** minimum wage/overtime claims (private releases generally invalid
    without DOL or court supervision);
  - **unemployment benefits** (NC DES eligibility can't be contracted away —
    but check how the agreement characterizes the separation; "resignation"
    language can hurt an unemployment claim);
  - workers' compensation claims (NC Industrial Commission approval required);
  - claims arising **after** the signing date (prospective waivers invalid);
  - vested ERISA benefits (401(k), vested pension).
- **Mutual vs one-way**: Is the release mutual? Usually it isn't; asking for a
  mutual release is a standard, low-cost negotiation win.

## 2. Money and benefits

- **NC Wage and Hour Act (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 95-25.1 et seq.)**:
  - Final wages due by the next regular payday — this is owed, not severance.
  - Accrued vacation/PTO must be paid out **unless** a written policy clearly
    provides for forfeiture. If the agreement is silent on PTO, ask where it
    went. **(defect check if agreement tries to net owed wages against severance)**
  - Commissions/bonuses: payable per the written policy in effect; ambiguity
    is resolved against the employer. Watch for "must be employed on payment
    date" conditions and whether termination timing was used to dodge a payout.
- **Severance amount**: benchmark against tenure and seniority (common market
  range: 1–4 weeks per year of service; executives often more). Lump sum vs
  installments — installments usually come with continued-compliance strings;
  identify them.
- **Section 409A**: severance paid over time can implicate deferred-comp tax
  rules; flag payment schedules crossing tax years for the user's tax advisor.
- **COBRA**: continuation coverage is a legal right regardless of the
  agreement. Is the employer subsidizing premiums? If not, that's a standard
  ask. Note the election deadline.
- **Equity**: unvested option/RSU treatment, exercise windows (often 90 days
  post-termination — a real money deadline), and whether the agreement
  accelerates, forfeits, or stays silent. Silence means the plan document
  controls — ask for it.
- **WARN Act**: mass layoff or plant closing at an employer with 100+
  employees requires 60 days' notice; pay in lieu is common. If the user was
  part of a large layoff with short notice, check whether severance is really
  WARN pay dressed up as generosity.

## 3. Restrictive covenants

- **NC non-compete law** — enforceable only if: in writing, part of an
  employment contract, supported by valuable consideration, reasonable in time
  and territory, and protecting a legitimate business interest.
  - **Consideration timing (defect check)**: continued employment alone is NOT
    valid consideration in NC for a covenant signed mid-employment — there
    must be new consideration (raise, promotion, bonus). A covenant signed at
    termination is supported by the severance itself, so this mostly bites
    covenants signed years earlier that the separation agreement "reaffirms."
  - **Strict blue-pencil doctrine (defect check)**: NC courts may strike
    distinctly separable unreasonable terms but will **not rewrite** an
    overbroad covenant. An overreaching territory or activity definition can
    void the whole restriction — flag overbreadth as leverage.
  - Reasonableness rough guide: ≤2 years and territory limited to where the
    employee actually worked/had customer contact is defensible; nationwide
    bans for non-executives, or bans on an entire industry, are vulnerable.
  - Federal backdrop: the FTC's attempted non-compete ban was challenged in
    court — verify current status before telling the user a covenant is or
    isn't federally barred.
- **Non-solicitation** (customers/employees): same NC reasonableness analysis;
  customer non-solicits limited to customers the employee actually served are
  the defensible core.
- **Confidentiality/NDA**:
  - **Defend Trade Secrets Act immunity notice**: agreements should preserve
    the right to disclose trade secrets confidentially to government agencies
    or in sealed court filings; absence is worth noting.
  - Perpetual confidentiality over non-trade-secret information is overbroad;
    watch definitions of "Confidential Information" that swallow the
    employee's general skills and knowledge.

## 4. Speech and cooperation clauses

- **NLRB limits (defect check for non-supervisory employees)**: under
  *McLaren Macomb* (2023), broad confidentiality and non-disparagement
  provisions in severance agreements can violate NLRA Section 7 for covered
  (non-supervisor) employees. Overbroad = leverage. Verify current Board
  position, as this area shifts with Board composition.
- **Speak Out Act (2022)**: pre-dispute NDAs can't bar disclosure of sexual
  assault/harassment allegations.
- **Non-disparagement**: is it mutual? Does it have carve-outs for truthful
  statements to government agencies, in legal proceedings, and (post-*McLaren*)
  protected concerted activity? One-way non-disparagement with no carve-outs
  is a standard 🔴.
- **Confidentiality of the agreement itself**: carve-outs should exist for
  spouse, attorney, tax advisor, and as required by law.
- **Cooperation clauses**: unlimited future cooperation with employer
  litigation is unpaid work — ask for reasonable-expenses reimbursement and
  an hourly rate after a cap.
- **Reference/rehire**: does the agreement lock in a neutral reference? A
  no-rehire clause is common but negotiable, and matters if the employer is
  dominant in the user's field or region.

## 5. Enforcement mechanics (the boilerplate that bites)

- **Governing law / forum**: NC employee should resist another state's law or
  a distant forum, especially for covenant disputes (choice of law can decide
  whether the strict NC blue-pencil rule applies).
- **Arbitration**: mandatory arbitration with class waiver — note what the
  employee gives up (jury, appeal, sometimes discovery). Check who pays
  arbitration costs. Note: the Ending Forced Arbitration Act (2022) lets
  employees invalidate pre-dispute arbitration clauses for sexual
  harassment/assault claims.
- **Fee-shifting**: one-way attorney's-fees clauses (employer recovers fees if
  it enforces, employee never does) are a 🔴; ask for mutual or removal.
- **Liquidated damages / clawback**: repay-all-severance-upon-any-breach
  clauses turn a trivial breach into total forfeiture — ask for materiality
  and cure-period language.
- **Integration clause**: kills every verbal promise ("we'll give you a good
  reference," "you can keep the laptop"). Anything promised must be in the
  document.
- **Return of property / final logistics**: deadlines, personal data on
  company devices, expense reimbursements still owed.

## 6. NC-specific extras

- **At-will backdrop**: NC is at-will; no severance is legally required, which
  frames what's negotiable (everything above the legal floor is a business
  decision by the employer, and they've already decided paying is worth it).
- **REDA (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 95-240 et seq.)**: retaliation claims tied to
  workers' comp, wage complaints, or OSHA activity — check the release list.
- **NC has no state WARN act and no state severance mandate**; federal WARN
  and the written policies/contract are the only floors.
- **State continuation coverage** exists for small-employer plans not covered
  by federal COBRA (employers under 20 employees) — relevant only for small
  employers.
